“Clinically tested” and “clinically proven” are not the same words

They get used interchangeably in supplement marketing, and the difference matters.

“Clinically tested” means a study was run. It does not, on its own, tell you what the study found, how many people were in it, whether there was a placebo group, or whether anyone independent looked at it.

The questions worth asking about any supplement study: Was it double-blind? Was it placebo-controlled? Where was it run, and is that institution independent? What exactly was measured, and for how long?

For GF-9, the answers are on the record: a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled crossover trial, run at Pennington Biomedical Research Center — an independent academic research institution — and registered publicly as NCT01540773. Sixteen participants. Growth hormone measured over a 120-minute window.

Small study, short window, and we are telling you both numbers rather than letting you find them somewhere else. That is the standard you should hold this category to — ours included.
